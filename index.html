🌿 Giving the Land
Back to Itself

Rewilding Canada restores wild landscapes one property at a time — removing fences, reviving streams, and letting nature lead.

A National Vision, Starting Here

Canada holds roughly 20% of the world's freshwater, nearly a quarter of its boreal forest, and some of the last intact temperate rainforest on earth. Yet decades of agricultural intensification, resource extraction, and urban sprawl have fragmented much of what remains. Rewilding Canada aims to change the relationship between landowners and the land beneath their feet.

We are starting with two private properties in Ravenna, Ontario — and we aim to grow. Our aspiration is to connect landowners, scientists, and communities across provinces around a shared understanding: healthy land is not idle land. It is the most productive thing any of us can steward.

Less management. More trust. Longer thinking. These are the principles guiding everything we do.

Land Acknowledgment

The lands we work on, and the lands we aspire to restore across Canada, are the ancestral territories of Indigenous peoples who have stewarded these ecosystems for thousands of years. We are guests here. Our work is informed by — and should always remain accountable to — Indigenous knowledge, leadership, and sovereignty over these landscapes.

🗺Rewilding Across Canada

Every Canadian province holds a distinct ecological story — and a distinct set of pressures. We aim to work across these differences, not despite them.

British Columbia
Temperate Rainforest & Salmon Rivers
Old-growth under pressure from logging. Salmon runs declining but recoverable. Rewilding here means defending what remains and restoring riparian corridors that connect mountain snowpack to the sea.
The Prairies
Lost Grasslands & Missing Bison
Less than 20% of native prairie remains. Bison reintroduction is underway in pockets. Regenerative agriculture partnerships could unlock enormous habitat potential across Saskatchewan and Manitoba.
Ontario & Quebec
Fragmented Forest, Recovering Wetlands
Southern Ontario is among the most ecologically fragmented regions in the country. The Niagara Escarpment — where we are starting — is one of the last continuous corridors. Wetland restoration is accelerating across the Shield.
The Maritimes
Atlantic Salmon & Acadian Forest
The Acadian forest — a rare mix of boreal and temperate species — is slowly recovering from centuries of clear-cutting. Atlantic salmon populations are a bellwether. Community-led watershed stewardship is showing real results.
The North
Boreal & Arctic — Still Largely Intact
The boreal and Arctic remain among Earth's most intact ecosystems — but permafrost thaw, resource corridors, and climate shifts are accelerating. Protecting what's intact is its own form of rewilding.

The goal isn't to return to a specific point in the past. It's to restore the processes — the fire, the flood, the predator, the seed — that allow the land to find its own complexity again.

Founder & Land Steward
George Knowles

George is the proprietor of two properties near Ravenna, Ontario — the Maxwell Property (located in the town of Maxwell) and the Ravenna Property — where Rewilding Canada's first active restoration work is underway. Since moving to the Niagara Escarpment, George's primary mission has been the preservation and sensitive restoration of the area's heritage structures, bringing old barns and stone buildings back to life rather than letting them fall.

That stewardship of built heritage has evolved into a deeper stewardship of the land itself. George initiated the rewilding work now documented on this site, and occasionally hosts community meetings in the restored barn — gathering neighbours, naturalists, and conservationists around the idea that the Escarpment landscape can be something more than what it currently is.

George is a member of the Escarpment Corridor Alliance, a regional coalition working to maintain and restore wildlife connectivity along the Niagara Escarpment.

🌎 What is
Rewilding?

Restoring Process,
Not Just Place

Rewilding is one of the most promising — and most misunderstood — ideas in modern conservation. It is not about returning to a specific historical moment. It is about restoring the processes that allow ecosystems to self-organise, recover, and flourish.

For most of human history, large-scale human activity was localised. Ecosystems had room to recover between disturbances. Predators kept prey populations in check. Rivers flooded and meandered. Forests burned and regrew. Wetlands stored water and filtered runoff. The land was dynamic, not static — and that dynamism created the biological richness we inherited.

Over the past two centuries, industrial agriculture, logging, urban expansion, and the suppression of natural processes have simplified landscapes dramatically. Many Canadian ecosystems now support a fraction of the species diversity they once did — not because the land is incapable of more, but because we have not given it the conditions to produce it.

Rewilding asks a different question: instead of asking what we should add to a damaged landscape, it asks what we should stop doing — and what we should gently restore — to allow natural recovery to take over.

🔗 Connectivity

Isolated habitat patches support far less biodiversity than connected ones. Rewilding prioritises creating or restoring landscape-scale corridors — along rivers, through hedgerows, across farm boundaries — that allow species to move, find mates, and recolonise lost habitat.

🐺 Trophic Complexity

Ecosystems need predators. Without them, prey populations balloon, overgraze, and simplify habitats. Rewilding works to restore trophic relationships — sometimes through the reintroduction of keystone species, sometimes simply by reducing human pressure enough for natural dynamics to reassert themselves.

🌊 Natural Processes

Fire, flood, wind-throw, beaver dams, and migratory grazing are all natural disturbances that ecosystems evolved around. Rewilding aims to restore these processes — or their functional equivalents — so that the land can generate its own complexity rather than depending on ongoing human management.

🌱 Passive Recovery

Sometimes the most powerful intervention is stopping an old one. When fields are no longer mowed, when fences come out, when streams are no longer channelised, nature fills the vacuum rapidly. Passive rewilding — simply stepping back — is often underestimated as a tool.

👥 Community Engagement

Rewilding at meaningful scales requires landowner cooperation, community buy-in, and Indigenous partnership. It is not a top-down conservation model. The most successful projects build the case locally — demonstrating what recovery looks like on one property, then inviting neighbours to join.

📊 Long-term Thinking

Ecosystems do not recover in years — they recover in decades and centuries. Rewilding requires a fundamentally different relationship with time. We are planting trees whose shade we will not sit in. That is exactly the point.

What Does Rewilding Look Like in Canada?

Canada's ecological diversity means rewilding takes different forms in different places. There is no single template. What follows are some of the approaches that resonate most strongly in the landscapes we work in and aspire to reach.

Niagara Escarpment · Ontario

Escarpment Corridor Restoration

The Escarpment is one of the last intact north-south wildlife corridors in southern Ontario. De-fencing farm margins, restoring stream hydrology, and reestablishing native forest at woodland edges can dramatically increase the biological value of private land that flanks the core protected area.

Georgian Bay · Ontario

Salmon Passage Restoration

Dozens of Escarpment tributaries that once supported Chinook and brook trout runs were channelised or culverted during agricultural intensification. Culvert removal and stream remeandering — already underway on several Grey County properties — allows fish to recolonise habitat that has been physically blocked for decades.

Southern Ontario · Carolinian Zone

Carolinian Forest Expansion

The Carolinian forest zone — which contains more species at risk than anywhere else in Canada — now covers less than 11% of its original area. Woodland expansion at farm edges, even on modest scales, contributes directly to a regional recovery network that larger conservation organisations cannot achieve alone.

Across Canada

Grassland & Prairie Recovery

Less than 20% of Canada's native grassland remains. In Saskatchewan and Manitoba, landowner-led initiatives to retire marginal cropland and allow native grasses to return are demonstrating that productive farming and meaningful ecological recovery are not mutually exclusive — they can reinforce each other.

British Columbia

Old-Growth Stewardship

Some of BC's remaining old-growth forests are on private land. Where landowners choose to protect rather than harvest, the ecological return — in carbon storage, species diversity, and watershed function — is among the highest of any conservation action available in Canada.

National

Bison & Keystone Species

Before settlement, bison shaped the entire prairie ecosystem through grazing, wallowing, and migration. Several First Nations-led bison restoration projects are underway. On the Escarpment, wolves moving through the corridor serve a comparable function — and their presence, however rare, signals ecosystem health.

Mammal

White-tailed Deer

Odocoileus virginianus

Seen daily on both properties. Their browsing patterns shift noticeably as cover and food sources evolve — a useful real-time indicator of how the landscape is changing.

Mammal

Coyote

Canis latrans

Regularly captured on trail camera crossing the north field edge. Their presence keeps rabbit and rodent populations balanced — essential for natural grassland regeneration on the old pasture areas.

Mammal

American Beaver

Castor canadensis

Chewed stumps and dam activity visible along the stream corridor downstream of the property boundary. Their natural engineering widens and deepens the channel we're working to restore — useful allies.

Mammal

Raccoon

Procyon lotor

Common along the stream corridor and around the barn at night. Their digging at pond margins can disturb turtle nests — something we keep an eye on near the Blanding's turtle sighting area.

Bird · Threatened

Barn Swallow

Hirundo rustica

Nesting in the barn — the primary reason it's staying standing. At least two active nests confirmed last season. Loft access gaps are maintained deliberately as part of the heritage structure stewardship work.

Bird

Wild Turkey

Meleagris gallopavo

A flock of 8–12 crosses the south field most mornings. Their scratching through leaf litter at the woodlot edge loosens soil and helps native seed work its way in — free tillage.

Bird

Ruffed Grouse

Bonasa umbellus

Drumming heard from the woodlot edge every spring. A species that thrives in exactly the kind of brushy, early-successional habitat that's forming naturally on the unmowed margins of both properties.

Bird

Cooper's Hawk

Accipiter cooperii

Hunts the woodlot edge and open fields year-round. One of the more dramatic regular sightings on the properties — often flushing the turkey flock from the south field on morning passes.

Fish · Species of Concern

Brook Trout

Salvelinus fontinalis

Present in the upper stream reach where summer water temperatures stay cold enough. A living benchmark for the stream revival work — if the riparian planting and channel restoration succeed, their range on the property should expand.

Amphibian

Spring Peeper

Pseudacris crucifer

The pond at the south property edge becomes deafening in April. We track chorus intensity each spring informally — a simple measure of whether the wetland is getting healthier from one year to the next.

Amphibian

American Bullfrog

Lithobates catesbeianus

Common in the pond and along stream margins. Their deep bass calls overlap with the peepers each spring. A prey source for herons, raccoons, and large snakes throughout the warmer months.

Reptile

Eastern Garter Snake

Thamnophis sirtalis sirtalis

Abundant — in the stone wall remnants, under debris piles, along the stream bank. Removing one of the old fence rows uncovered a small communal basking spot we've since left undisturbed.

Reptile · Threatened

Blanding's Turtle

Emydoidea blandingii

One individual confirmed at the pond edge in June — a significant find. Threatened in Ontario and very slow to recover from losses. The pond margin here appears to be suitable nesting habitat; raccoon activity in that area is being watched closely.

Insect · Endangered

Monarch Butterfly

Danaus plexippus

Seen reliably in late summer on the milkweed along the old fence line — one of our reasons for leaving that margin unmowed. Expanding milkweed into the south field is a direct priority in the native species work.

Insect

Bumble Bees

Bombus spp.

Multiple species foraging across both properties through the season. Ground-nesting populations benefit directly from the areas we've stopped mowing — an easy win that costs nothing.

Two existing individuals on the property — both healthy, which is increasingly rare. Provincially protected. We're planting disease-resistant seedlings from a certified Escarpment-region source to expand the population on site.

Tree

Yellow Birch

Several mature individuals at the woodlot centre, distinguished by their peeling golden bark. A long-lived component of the forest here. Seeds carry finches and siskins through the winter months.

Shrub

Red-osier Dogwood

Already established along the stream bank — brilliant red stems through all winter. A top choice for stream bank stabilization planting; berries feed migrating birds heavily in late summer.

Shrub

Common Elderberry

Sambucus canadensis

Naturalized along the ditch margin. Berries consumed by over 50 bird species — one of the highest-wildlife-value shrubs native to this region. We're propagating cuttings to establish additional stands on the property.

Wildflower

Common Milkweed

Asclepias syriaca

Growing along the unmowed fence line margins — preserved deliberately as the sole larval host plant for Monarch butterflies. We're seeding additional milkweed into the south field margins this season.

Wildflower

Wild Columbine

Aquilegia canadensis

Established on the rocky north-facing slope. The first significant nectar source of the year for hummingbirds returning from migration. Spreading on its own — no help needed here.

Wildflower

Canada Goldenrod

Solidago canadensis

Filling the unmowed field margins brilliantly come September. Supports over 100 insect species and seeds finches through winter. Often mistakenly blamed for hay fever — that's ragweed, which flowers at the same time.

Fern

Ostrich Fern

Matteuccia struthiopteris

Dense colonies at the shaded stream bank. Divisions are being transplanted upstream to stabilize bare eroded sections ahead of the more involved channel restoration work.

Invasive · Removal Ongoing

Common Buckthorn

Rhamnus cathartica

The primary invasive problem on both properties — particularly dense along the old hedgerows. Cut stumps are treated on the spot. Birds continuously reintroduce seed from neighbouring properties, so this is a permanent, multi-year commitment.

Invasive · Removal Ongoing

Dog-strangling Vine

Vincetoxicum rossicum

A significant infestation along the south field margin. Particularly destructive because it lures Monarchs into laying eggs on it — where the caterpillars cannot survive. Hand-pulling every season before it sets seed.

Invasive · Removal Ongoing

Garlic Mustard

Alliaria petiolata

Spreading through the woodlot understory. Releases chemicals that inhibit the mycorrhizal fungi native tree seedlings depend on. Early spring pulling before it sets seed is the most effective approach — and the timing is unforgiving.

🛠On the Ground

Active restoration work across two properties near Ravenna, Ontario. Each property has its own character, its own pressures, and its own set of projects — but both are part of the same long-term vision.

🌊 The Maxwell Property
Maxwell, Ontario · Stream corridor · Mixed forest & open field · Year 2

🌊

Stream Revival

The drainage ditch cutting through the Maxwell Property was straightened decades ago for field drainage — too shallow, too fast, and too warm for the brook trout that survive only in the coldest upper reach. Work involves removing a failed culvert blocking fish passage, adding woody debris and root structure to deepen pools, and planting a 50-metre riparian buffer of cedar, dogwood, and ostrich fern along eroded banks. No machinery — everything by hand over multiple seasons.

Reintroducing Native Species

Native tree seedlings — sugar maple, white cedar, butternut, trembling aspen, yellow birch — planted at the woodland edges following existing forest contours rather than in rows. Milkweed and goldenrod seeded into unmowed field margins. The goal is extension of existing woodland, not isolated plantation plantings.

Common buckthorn, garlic mustard, and dog-strangling vine are all present on the Maxwell Property and actively being removed. Cut stumps treated on site. Dog-strangling vine hand-pulled each spring before seed set. This is a permanent, multi-year commitment — seed rain from neighbouring properties means it never fully stops.

🏚 The Ravenna Property
Ravenna, Ontario · Heritage barn · Hedgerow & field mosaic · Year 2
▼
🪧

De-Fencing

Agricultural wire fencing that hasn't held livestock in years cuts across the field edge, through the woodlot, and across what was once continuous hedgerow. We're pulling all 10km of it — removing staples and wire by hand, rolling and salvaging the material, leaving posts as habitat structure where they're not rotted through. Once the wire comes out, movement restores itself.

Active
10km total
🏚

Heritage Structure Stewardship

The barn on the Ravenna Property has active barn swallow nests in the loft and bat colonies in the wall gaps — species in real decline across Ontario. The approach is targeted stabilization only: reinforcing the frame where it's failing, maintaining or opening specific access points for swallows and bats, and leaving the building's character intact. George also hosts occasional community and conservation meetings in the barn, continuing its life as a gathering place.

Reintroducing Native Species

Milkweed seeded into south field margins to support Monarch butterfly populations. Elderberry and red-osier dogwood cuttings propagated from existing property stock and planted along hedgerow margins. Native wildflower mixes seeded into areas where mowing has been discontinued. No off-property inputs — everything sourced or propagated on site.

Documenting What's Here

Trail cameras placed at the fence removal sites and woodland edges are capturing the first systematic wildlife record for the Ravenna Property. One Blanding's Turtle confirmed at the pond margin — a significant find for a threatened species. All sightings logged to iNaturalist. This documentation will anchor the restoration case for years to come.

♻️

Reuse Over Replace

Fence posts pulled from de-fencing are stacked into brush piles and habitat structures rather than removed. Stone moved during restoration becomes reptile basking structures. Invasive shrubs cleared from hedgerows become nurse cover for native seedlings planted in their place. Nothing leaves the property if it can serve a purpose here.
