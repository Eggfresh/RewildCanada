About This Project

This is a personal land stewardship project on two privately owned properties near Ravenna, Ontario. The goal is simple: undo what's been done, and let the land heal. No grand plan — just steady, patient work guided by what nature is already trying to do on its own.

Land Acknowledgment

This land sits within the traditional territory of the Anishinaabek, Haudenosaunee, and Métis peoples. We are grateful to work here, and to continue a relationship with this land that began thousands of years before the fences went in.

🌊

Stream Revival

The small watercourses crossing these properties have been squeezed, straightened, and culverted over decades of farming. The work: removing fish barriers, deepening pools, and planting native riparian buffers — giving the water room to move the way it naturally wants to.

Hydrology
🪧

De-Fencing

Old agricultural wire cuts these fields into fragments that haven't held livestock in years. It snags deer and blocks the natural movement of nearly everything. We're pulling it out post by post — salvaging the wire rather than leaving it to rust into the soil.

Habitat Connectivity
🌱

Reintroducing Native Species

250 native tree seedlings planted at woodland edges to extend forest cover naturally. Milkweed and native wildflower mixes seeded into old field margins. Invasive species removed systematically to give native plants room to establish.

Ecological Restoration
🏚

Heritage Structure Stewardship

The old barn on the south property shelters barn swallows, bats, and a host of insect species in its rafters and wall gaps. Rather than let it fall or tear it down, we're stabilizing what matters structurally and adapting what remains for the wildlife that already calls it home.

Adaptive Stewardship
♻️

Reuse Over Replace

When fence posts come out, they become habitat piles. When invasive shrubs are cleared, the brush becomes nurse cover for new seedlings. When stone is moved, it becomes reptile basking structure. Nothing leaves these properties if it can serve a purpose here.

Circular Practice
📋

Documenting What's Here

Before we can restore it, we have to know it. We're building a running record of every species observed on both properties using iNaturalist and trail cameras. The wildlife and plant lists on this site reflect what we've actually seen — not what should theoretically be here.

Field Observation

We didn't start this to save the world. We started because we looked out at a field full of rusty wire and old fence posts and thought — what if we just took it all out and waited to see what came back?

Species confirmed on or directly adjacent to the two Ravenna properties — documented by trail camera, field observation, and iNaturalist. This list grows as we spend more time looking.

Mammal

White-tailed Deer

Odocoileus virginianus

Seen daily on both properties. Their browsing patterns shift noticeably as cover and food sources evolve — a useful real-time indicator of how the landscape is changing.

Mammal

Coyote

Canis latrans

Regularly captured on trail camera crossing the north field edge. Their presence keeps rabbit and rodent populations balanced — essential for natural grassland regeneration on the old pasture areas.

Mammal

American Beaver

Castor canadensis

Chewed stumps and dam activity visible along the stream corridor downstream of the property boundary. Their natural engineering widens and deepens the channel we're working to restore — useful allies.

Mammal

Raccoon

Procyon lotor

Common along the stream corridor and around the barn at night. Their digging at pond margins can disturb turtle nests — something we keep an eye on near the Blanding's turtle sighting area.

Bird · Threatened

Barn Swallow

Hirundo rustica

Nesting in the barn — the primary reason it's staying standing. At least two active nests confirmed last season. Loft access gaps are maintained deliberately as part of the heritage structure stewardship work.

Bird

Wild Turkey

Meleagris gallopavo

A flock of 8–12 crosses the south field most mornings. Their scratching through leaf litter at the woodlot edge loosens soil and helps native seed work its way in — free tillage.

Bird

Ruffed Grouse

Bonasa umbellus

Drumming heard from the woodlot edge every spring. A species that thrives in exactly the kind of brushy, early-successional habitat that's forming naturally on the unmowed margins of both properties.

Bird

Cooper's Hawk

Accipiter cooperii

Hunts the woodlot edge and open fields year-round. One of the more dramatic regular sightings on the properties — often flushing the turkey flock from the south field on morning passes.

Fish · Species of Concern

Brook Trout

Salvelinus fontinalis

Present in the upper stream reach where summer water temperatures stay cold enough. A living benchmark for the stream revival work — if the riparian planting and channel restoration succeed, their range on the property should expand.

Amphibian

Spring Peeper

Pseudacris crucifer

The pond at the south property edge becomes deafening in April. We track chorus intensity each spring informally — a simple measure of whether the wetland is getting healthier from one year to the next.

Amphibian

American Bullfrog

Lithobates catesbeianus

Common in the pond and along stream margins. Their deep bass calls overlap with the peepers each spring. A prey source for herons, raccoons, and large snakes throughout the warmer months.

Reptile

Eastern Garter Snake

Thamnophis sirtalis sirtalis

Abundant — in the stone wall remnants, under debris piles, along the stream bank. Removing one of the old fence rows uncovered a small communal basking spot we've since left undisturbed.

Reptile · Threatened

Blanding's Turtle

Emydoidea blandingii

One individual confirmed at the pond edge in June — a significant find. Threatened in Ontario and very slow to recover from losses. The pond margin here appears to be suitable nesting habitat; raccoon activity in that area is being watched closely.

Insect · Endangered

Monarch Butterfly

Danaus plexippus

Seen reliably in late summer on the milkweed along the old fence line — one of our reasons for leaving that margin unmowed. Expanding milkweed into the south field is a direct priority in the native species work.

Insect

Bumble Bees

Bombus spp.

Multiple species foraging across both properties through the season. Ground-nesting populations benefit directly from the areas we've stopped mowing — an easy win that costs nothing.

Plants on the
Properties

Native trees, shrubs, wildflowers, and ferns documented on or planted across the two Ravenna properties. Invasive species are flagged — removal is ongoing every season.

Common — established on property
At Risk — provincially or federally listed
Rare — single or very few individuals
Invasive — active removal underway
Tree

Sugar Maple

Acer saccharum

Dominant in the existing woodlot. One hundred seedlings planted at the forest edge this season — extending the woodlot into the field rather than creating isolated plantings out in the open.

Tree

Eastern White Cedar

Thuja occidentalis

Scattered naturally along the stream corridor and north-facing slope. Year-round thermal cover for deer and nesting birds. We're adding nursery-grown seedlings along the stream riparian buffer planting.

Tree

Trembling Aspen

Populus tremuloides

Already colonizing the field corners on its own — exactly what we want. One of the most ecologically valuable early-successional trees for insects and birds. We're leaving every volunteer completely alone.

Tree · Endangered

Butternut

Juglans cinerea

Two existing individuals on the property — both healthy, which is increasingly rare. Provincially protected. We're planting disease-resistant seedlings from a certified Escarpment-region source to expand the population on site.

Tree

Yellow Birch

Betula alleghaniensis

Several mature individuals at the woodlot centre, distinguished by their peeling golden bark. A long-lived component of the forest here. Seeds carry finches and siskins through the winter months.

Shrub

Red-osier Dogwood

Cornus sericea

Already established along the stream bank — brilliant red stems through all winter. A top choice for stream bank stabilization planting; berries feed migrating birds heavily in late summer.

Shrub

Common Elderberry

Sambucus canadensis

Naturalized along the ditch margin. Berries consumed by over 50 bird species — one of the highest-wildlife-value shrubs native to this region. We're propagating cuttings to establish additional stands on the property.

Wildflower

Common Milkweed

Asclepias syriaca

Growing along the unmowed fence line margins — preserved deliberately as the sole larval host plant for Monarch butterflies. We're seeding additional milkweed into the south field margins this season.

Wildflower

Wild Columbine

Aquilegia canadensis

Established on the rocky north-facing slope. The first significant nectar source of the year for hummingbirds returning from migration. Spreading on its own — no help needed here.

Wildflower

Canada Goldenrod

Solidago canadensis

Filling the unmowed field margins brilliantly come September. Supports over 100 insect species and seeds finches through winter. Often mistakenly blamed for hay fever — that's ragweed, which flowers at the same time.

Fern

Ostrich Fern

Matteuccia struthiopteris

Dense colonies at the shaded stream bank. Divisions are being transplanted upstream to stabilize bare eroded sections ahead of the more involved channel restoration work.

Invasive · Removal Ongoing

Common Buckthorn

Rhamnus cathartica

The primary invasive problem on both properties — particularly dense along the old hedgerows. Cut stumps are treated on the spot. Birds continuously reintroduce seed from neighbouring properties, so this is a permanent, multi-year commitment.

Invasive · Removal Ongoing

Dog-strangling Vine

Vincetoxicum rossicum

A significant infestation along the south field margin. Particularly destructive because it lures Monarchs into laying eggs on it — where the caterpillars cannot survive. Hand-pulling every season before it sets seed.

Invasive · Removal Ongoing

Garlic Mustard

Alliaria petiolata

Spreading through the woodlot understory. Releases chemicals that inhibit the mycorrhizal fungi native tree seedlings depend on. Early spring pulling before it sets seed is the most effective approach — and the timing is unforgiving.

On the Ground

Four projects currently underway across the two Ravenna properties. All work is done by hand, at our own pace, and documented as we go.

🌊

Stream Revival

The drainage ditch cutting through the Maxwell Property was straightened decades ago for field drainage — it's too shallow, too fast, and too warm for the brook trout that survive only in the coldest upper reach. The work involves removing a failed undersized culvert that's blocking fish passage, adding woody debris and root structure to slow flow and deepen pools, and planting a 50-metre riparian buffer of cedar, dogwood, and ostrich fern along the most eroded banks. No machinery — everything done by hand over multiple seasons.

Active
Maxwell Property · ~200m reach
🪚

De-Fencing

Both properties carry several hundred metres of agricultural wire fencing that hasn't served a livestock purpose in years. It cuts across the field edge, through the woodlot, and across what was once continuous hedgerow. We're pulling all of it — removing staples and wire by hand, rolling and salvaging the material, and leaving posts standing as habitat structure where they're not rotted through. Once the wire comes out, the field reconnects and movement restores itself.

Active
Both properties · 10km total
🌱

Reintroducing Native Species

250 native tree seedlings — sugar maple, white cedar, butternut, trembling aspen, yellow birch — planted at the woodland edges of both properties following the existing forest contour rather than in rows. Milkweed seed into the south field margins. Goldenrod and native aster into unmowed areas. Buckthorn, garlic mustard, and dog-strangling vine being removed aggressively to open space for what belongs here. This is ongoing work, not a one-time event.

Active
250 trees · both properties
🏚

Heritage Structure Stewardship

The barn on the south property has active barn swallow nests in the loft and almost certainly bat colonies in the wall gaps — species that are in real decline across Ontario and depend on exactly these kinds of old structures. The approach is targeted stabilization only: reinforcing the frame where it's failing, maintaining or opening specific access points for swallows and bats, and otherwise leaving the building's character intact. No restoration to former glory; just keeping a working habitat standing for the wildlife that has already claimed it.

Where We Are

Both properties are located near Ravenna in Collingwood Township, Grey County — within the Niagara Escarpment UNESCO World Biosphere Reserve. A landscape that once supported far more biological complexity than it does today. We're trying to bring a small piece of it back, starting here.
